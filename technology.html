Technology & product

At the core of RaceVision AI is a suite of computer‑vision models built to recognise individual horses in video footage. The models analyse racing silks (the colours and patterns worn by jockeys) and biometric markers to reliably identify each runner. Position tracking follows horses throughout the race, recording sectional times and relative positioning at each stage. An automated editing system then compiles clips that highlight recent performances, closing speed, preferred running lanes and tactical patterns.

The platform is hosted on scalable cloud infrastructure to handle high‑definition video streams and intensive processing workloads. An intuitive web interface allows users to request custom highlight reels on demand. Over time, machine‑learning algorithms will learn which metrics matter most to different punter profiles and personalise the way information is presented.

How we unlock the opportunity

To build the foundation, we will partner with racing broadcasters and data providers to access live and archived footage under licensing agreements. A comprehensive database of racing colours and horse identifiers will be created to train the recognition models. Automated video‑editing pipelines will assemble highlight reels based on performance criteria such as fastest closing 400 metres, change of position and response to pressure.

We will launch in major racing jurisdictions with extensive video archives and work closely with professional punters and racing analysts as beta testers. Their feedback will inform feature development and ensure the product reflects on‑track realities. The platform will expand to new territories as standardised video feeds become available.

Our technology roadmap includes adding additional sensor inputs—such as GPS trackers and heart‑rate monitors—when available to enrich the analytics. We also plan to explore multi‑camera array processing, similar to systems used in equine health monitoring, to provide three‑dimensional reconstructions of racing lines and stride patterns. Continuous model training and human‑in‑the‑loop validation will ensure the AI remains accurate across varying track conditions and jurisdictions.

Development roadmap

Building RaceVision AI is a staged process designed to deliver value early while steadily expanding the system’s capabilities. Our development plan is as follows:

  • Detection engine (months 1–4): Train and deploy models to recognise individual horses using racing silks and biometric markers. Begin ingesting video feeds from initial jurisdictions and validate accuracy against ground‑truth data.
  • Video‑editing engine (months 4–10): Develop algorithms to automatically segment races into key phases (start, mid‑race, closing stages) and compile highlight reels based on criteria such as fastest splits, change of position and response to pressure.
  • Dashboard & user interface (months 8–12): Build a web‑based dashboard that allows users to search for a horse, filter by track or distance and generate custom form guides. Incorporate charts, tables and annotations to make the insights intuitive.
  • Beta testing & early revenue (months 6–12): Release a functional beta to a select group of professional punters, trainers and bookmakers. Charge subscription fees to validate the pricing model and incorporate feedback into product refinements.
  • Public launch (month 12+): Scale the platform across key racing territories, supported by marketing campaigns and partnership roll‑outs. Continue to refine models and add features based on user demand.

Potential technology partners

RaceVision AI relies on high‑performance computing, cloud storage and advanced AI tooling. Beyond direct investors from within the racing ecosystem, we see strategic alignment with global technology firms that are investing heavily in artificial‑intelligence infrastructure. Companies such as Nvidia, Microsoft and IBM provide the graphics‑processing units, cloud platforms and AI toolkits that our service depends on. These vendors are actively seeking to support applications that drive demand for their hardware and cloud services; partnering with or investing in RaceVision AI would showcase their technologies in a compelling, high‑volume use case.

We will explore co‑marketing arrangements, preferential pricing and potential equity participation with these suppliers. Their involvement could accelerate our development, reduce infrastructure costs and lend credibility to our AI capabilities. In return, RaceVision AI would generate significant cloud workloads and demonstrate how cutting‑edge video intelligence can unlock new verticals for these providers.
