Financials & projections

We are seeking to raise £750 k in seed capital to fund the development of RaceVision AI. Proceeds will cover computer‑vision research, scalable cloud infrastructure, video‑rights licensing, management talent and initial market penetration. A fully funded plan allows us to launch a revenue‑generating beta within six months and a public release within a year. Our financing strategy assumes a pre‑money valuation of around £3 million, implying that the seed investor would acquire about 20 % of the company for their investment.

The addressable market for horse‑racing intelligence is substantial. Major bookmakers collectively service tens of millions of customers, yet only a small fraction are serious punters. We estimate there are roughly 0.5–1 million professional or semi‑professional racing punters globally. Under our medium scenario, reaching 10,000 paying subscribers by year 3 equates to approximately 1–2 % of that base. With pricing around £200 per month and complementary enterprise licensing, pay‑per‑race and advertising revenues, our financial model projects annual income in the tens of millions by year 3 and approaching the £60–120 million range by year five. Because our service is delivered digitally, incremental subscribers add minimal cost and drive strong margins.

Projected P&L scenarios

To illustrate the medium‑term return potential, the table below outlines three scenarios over a five‑year horizon. Each case reflects different rates of subscriber adoption, enterprise licensing and advertising uptake. Operating costs scale with usage but benefit from economies of scale as the platform matures.

Scenario Year 3 revenue Year 3 net profit Year 5 revenue Year 5 net profit
Low case £20 million £4 million £40 million £12 million
Medium case £60 million £15 million £120 million £30 million
High case £120 million £30 million £250 million £70 million

Assumptions: The low case assumes around 10,000 professional subscribers by year 3 and 20,000 by year 5, paying on average £200 per month. It also includes five enterprise partners paying roughly £250 k each per year and modest pay‑per‑race and advertising revenue. The medium case models 30,000 subscribers growing to 60,000, plus 20 enterprise partners, a growing pay‑per‑race user base and steady sponsorship sales. The high case envisions 60,000 subscribers by year 3 rising to 120,000 by year 5, complemented by 50 partners and strong B2B licensing and advertising revenue. Operating margins improve from roughly 25 % in the early years to more than 40 % as fixed costs are leveraged. Under the medium scenario the initial investment could be repaid within three years, with substantial upside thereafter.

Cost considerations: These scenarios incorporate sizeable ongoing operating costs. Processing and storing thousands of high‑definition race videos require significant cloud compute and bandwidth, which we estimate at 20–25 % of revenue. Licensing and ingesting new footage account for a further 10–15 %, while marketing, customer support and continuous analytics development add around 10 %. Because subscription and enterprise revenues begin immediately with the beta launch, the business is designed to be revenue generating and largely self‑funding early on, with costs scaling proportionally as uptake grows.

Risks & mitigation

Our business faces a number of execution and regulatory risks. These include delays or difficulties in securing video rights, sudden changes in gambling regulation, technical challenges in maintaining model accuracy across varied track conditions, adoption resistance from punters accustomed to traditional form guides and the entry of well‑financed competitors. We will mitigate these risks by:

  • Diversifying data partnerships: Securing licensing agreements with multiple broadcasters and jurisdictions so that our service is not reliant on a single rights holder.
  • Maintaining regulatory compliance: Hiring legal experts to monitor gambling and data‑privacy laws and adapting our product as regulations evolve. We will incorporate responsible‑gaming tools and age verification where required.
  • Continual model improvement: Implementing a feedback loop with professional users to flag misidentifications or anomalies, and retraining models regularly to accommodate different camera angles, lighting conditions and racing jurisdictions.
  • User education & marketing: Providing tutorials and ambassador‑led content to help punters understand and trust AI‑driven analysis, easing the transition from traditional form guides.
  • Building first‑mover advantage: Moving quickly to establish partnerships and brand recognition, making it harder for new entrants to displace us. A scalable, cloud‑based architecture will allow us to onboard customers rapidly.

Exit strategies

There are multiple ways to realise value for investors once the platform achieves traction. Potential exit scenarios include:

  • Trade sale to a bookmaker or exchange: Large wagering operators constantly seek proprietary analytics and differentiated content to attract customers. Acquiring RaceVision AI would provide them with a competitive edge.
  • Acquisition by a sports‑data or media company: Rights holders and broadcasters increasingly invest in data and analysis platforms. Our service would complement their content portfolios and open new revenue streams.
  • Strategic investment from an analytics or technology firm: Companies in AI or cloud computing may acquire or invest in RaceVision AI to enter the racing market or showcase their technology.
  • Management buy‑out or IPO: Should the business scale rapidly and achieve strong profitability, a public listing or management buy‑out could provide liquidity while retaining independence.
